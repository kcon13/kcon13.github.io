Research

My research primarily centers on supporting healthier aging experiences for Native American Elders.

As a statistician, my work spans two interconnected areas: developing and assessing statistical methodology, and applying those methods to real-world data. My methodological research focuses on how assumptions and data structures influence results in time‑to‑event models, particularly within observational Indigenous healthcare settings.

I explore two central questions:

  1. How do complexities in data collection and structure affect point estimates and predictive performance?
  2. How do implicit modeling assumptions shape results, and which assumptions remain most robust when those assumptions are misspecified?

On the applied side, my work examines factors related to dementia diagnoses, disease progression, and research participation among American Indian and Alaska Native (AI/AN) individuals. This includes social determinants of health, biomarkers, and demographic characteristics.

Gleason Lab, UW-Madison

The Gleason lab at UW-Madison is led by Dr. Carey Gleason (Professor of Geriatrics and Gerontology) focuses on improving the diagnosis and treatment of Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias (ADRD) for all individuals, including women, African Americans, Native Americans, and other groups traditionally under-represented in research.

Grillen Lab, UCI

The Grillen lab at UCI is led by Dr. Joshua Grill (Professor of Psychiatry & Human Behavior and Neurobiology & Behavior, Associate Director ADRC, Director UCI MIND) and Dr. Daniel L. Gillen (Professor and Chair of Statistics, Director ADRC Data Management and Statistics Core). The group specializes in diversity, retention, recruitment, clinical trials, and adverse events Alzheimer's disease and related dementias. The lab provides a collaborative environment for early-career researchers, postdocs, and graduate students to develop skills in ADRD research and applied statistical methods.

Memory Keepers Medical Discovery Team, University of Minnesota

The Memory Keepers Medical Discovery Team conducts community‑engaged research to improve dementia outcomes in Indigenous and rural communities. Led by Dr. Kristen Jacklin, the team works to preserve brain health through partnerships with tribal communities, rural health systems, and large‑scale data initiatives. Their long‑term goal is to advance brain health equity and improve quality of life for individuals and families affected by dementia.

Center for American Indian and Alaska Native Health (CAIANH)

The Center for American Indian and Alaska Native Health conducts research to understand dementia in AI/AN communities through both pre‑dementia and post‑diagnosis pathways. Their work examines sociodemographic and modifiable risk factors associated with dementia onset—particularly the role of diabetes—as well as changes in healthcare utilization, treatment effectiveness, and costs after diagnosis. My collaboration supported statistical analyses aimed at identifying disparities and improving dementia‑related care for AI/AN populations.

Statistical Methodology and Applied Research Talks (SMART) Group, UCI

The SMART Group, led by Dr. Daniel Gillen, is a weekly seminar series where graduate students, postdocs, and faculty present ongoing methodological and applied research. The group provides a space to share work in progress, ask statistical questions, and broaden exposure to diverse research topics within the UCI Statistics Department. My involvement included presenting my own work, contributing to discussions, and engaging with peers on methodological challenges.

Artificial Intelligence/Machine Learning Consortium for Advancing Health Equity and Research Diversity (AIM-AHEAD)

As a stakeholder for the Midwest and South Central region, my work with AIM‑AHEAD aligns closely with my dissertation and CAIANH collaborations. The consortium aims to develop artificial intelligence and machine learning tools that advance health equity for communities traditionally excluded in research and healthcare. On Semi-Parametric Regression for Time-to-Event Analyses in Electronic Health Records Studies

My dissertation focused on improving the robustness of time‑to‑event analyses for dementia‑related outcomes, motivated by observational data from Native American healthcare systems and the health disparities present in these settings. The work is organized into three projects.

  1. System Migration in Electronic Health Records

    2. The first project examines how system migration—patients receiving care across multiple healthcare systems—affects time‑to‑event analyses when only one system’s data are available. Missing diagnoses from outside systems can introduce substantial bias, especially under proportional hazards models. This project develops methods to identify patients likely to be receiving care elsewhere and evaluates strategies to reduce bias when diagnoses occur in unobserved systems before appearing in the observed one.

  2. Extending Time-Dependent AUROC to Recurrent Events

    3. The second project extends time‑dependent ROC and AUROC methods to recurrent event settings. Standard approaches treat each event as independent, causing individuals with more events to contribute disproportionately to the AUROC and biasing prediction models toward those subpopulations. This work introduces a reweighting approach that ensures each individual contributes equally, improving fairness and interpretability in recurrent event prediction.

  3. Time Scale Selection in Survival Models

    4. The final project is a simulation study evaluating how different time scales influence bias and variance in estimated covariate effects. The study considers three scenarios:

    1. Misspecification of the time variable
    2. Misspecification of the covariate effect
    3. Violations of the proportional hazards assumption

    Although previous work has explored time scale choice in observational studies, simulation results have been inconsistent. This project clarifies those inconsistencies and provides guidance for selecting appropriate time scales, particularly for end‑of‑life conditions such as Alzheimer’s disease and related dementias.

Applied Research

Projects focused on real‑world health data, with an emphasis on Indigenous health, research participation, and dementia‑related outcomes.

My applied research is focused on understanding and improving the health of Indigenous populations worldwide. I have experience in research participant, biomarker access, resilience factors, modifiable risk factors, and disease progression among Indigenous populations and communities.
